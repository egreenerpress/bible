Proverbs Chapter 25

1 These are additional proverbs of Solomon, which the officials of King Hezekiah of Judah transcribed.

2 It is God's nature to keep certain things hidden, but it is the duty and privilege of rulers to diligently seek out and uncover the truth.

3 The heavens are vast in their height, and the earth is profound in its depth, but the heart of a king is beyond understanding.

4 Remove the impurities from the silver, and a pure vessel will emerge, fit for the craftsman's use.

5 Remove the wicked from the presence of the king, and his rule will be firmly established through justice.

6 Do not exalt yourself in the presence of the king, and do not take a position among those of high rank.

7 It is far better for someone to say to you, "Come up here," than for you to be humiliated and moved to a lower place in the presence of the ruler whom you have seen with your own eyes.

8 Do not rush into a dispute, for you may not know how to resolve it in the end, especially when your neighbor humiliates you.

9 Present your case directly to your neighbor, and do not reveal someone else's confidential matter to others.

10 Be careful, or the one who hears it may bring shame upon you, and your disgrace will never be forgotten.

11 A well-timed and appropriate word is like golden apples set in a framework of silver.

12 Like a gold earring or a finely crafted ornament, so is the correction of a wise person when given to a listening ear.

13 Just as the refreshing coolness of snow during the harvest season brings relief, so is a trustworthy messenger to those who send him: for he brings comfort and renewal to the hearts of his masters.

14 Whoever boasts about a gift they do not truly possess is like clouds and wind that promise rain but bring none.

15 A ruler can be won over through patient persuasion, and gentle words have the power to resolve even the most hardened resistance.

16 Have you found honey? Eat only what is enough for you, so that you do not overindulge and become sick from it.

17 Do not overstay your welcome at your neighbor's house, or they may grow tired of you and come to resent you.

18 A person who gives false testimony against their neighbor is like a crushing hammer, a deadly sword, and a piercing arrow.

19 Relying on an untrustworthy person in times of trouble is as useless as a broken tooth or a dislocated foot—it only causes more pain and leaves you unable to function.

20 Just as taking away a garment in cold weather leaves one exposed to the chill, and like pouring vinegar on soda causes a useless reaction, so is the one who sings cheerful songs to a person with a heavy heart—it is out of place and brings no comfort.

21 If your enemy is hungry, give him food to eat; and if he is thirsty, give him water to drink.

22 For you will heap burning coals upon his head, and the LORD will honor you.

23 The north wind drives away the rain, and in the same way, a stern expression silences a slanderous tongue.

24 It is better to live in a small corner on the roof than to share a spacious house with a quarrelsome and contentious woman.

25 Just as cool water refreshes a parched spirit, so does good news bring joy when it comes from a distant land.

26 A righteous person who falters in the presence of the wicked is like a polluted well or a contaminated spring.

27 Eating too much honey is not good for you, and in the same way, seeking your own honor brings no true honor.

28 A person who lacks self-control is like a city with broken-down walls, left defenseless and vulnerable.
